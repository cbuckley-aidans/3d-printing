TEST + ITERATE: PREPARATION & METHODS

Welcome to the Test and Iterate phase! This is where you test your prototype, learn what works (and what doesn't), and make improvements. It's all about learning and making your design better through cycles of testing and changing.

This section helps you prepare for testing by choosing methods, thinking about tips, and planning documentation for Workbook Page 15.

1

Choosing Iteration Methods (Page 15)

How will you test your prototype and figure out what needs changing? Page 15 lists different methods. Choose the ones that make sense for your project and tick them off.

A

Testing & Evaluation Methods Explained

User Testing

What: Watching your actual end user (or someone similar) try to use your prototype to complete a task.

How: Prepare your prototype. Ask the user to perform a specific task using it (e.g., "Try opening this jar using the device"). Observe them closely: Where do they struggle? What do they do easily? Ask open-ended questions afterwards ("What did you think?", "Was anything confusing?"). Record their actions and feedback.

Why: The BEST way to see if your design actually works for the intended person. Provides direct, real-world feedback.

Roleplay / Simulation

What: You or your teammates try to use the prototype while pretending to have the user's challenges or limitations.

How: If your user has limited hand strength, maybe try using the prototype while wearing thick gloves. If they have vision impairment, try using it with blurry glasses (safely!). Act out the scenario the prototype is designed for.

Why: Helps you empathise and spot potential physical challenges, especially if direct user testing is difficult to arrange frequently.

Survey

What: Creating a set of written questions to ask people (maybe the user, their family, or even classmates for general feedback) about the prototype.

How: Write clear questions. Use rating scales (e.g., "On a scale of 1-5, how easy was it to use?") or open-ended questions ("What did you like most?"). Distribute the survey and collect responses.

Why: Good for gathering opinions from multiple people or getting feedback on specific aspects like appearance or perceived ease of use.

Evaluation Matrix

What: A table or chart where you rate your prototype against the Design Criteria you set earlier (e.g., Must Haves, Should Haves from Page 11).

How: List your key criteria down one side of a table. Create columns for scoring (e.g., "Doesn't Meet", "Partly Meets", "Fully Meets" or 1-5 score). Test your prototype yourself or with a user, then objectively score how well it meets each criterion.

Why: Provides a structured way to see if your design meets its goals. Helps identify specific areas needing improvement.

Importance Difficulty Matrix

What: A chart used to decide which feedback points or potential changes to focus on next. You plot items based on how important they are vs. how difficult they are to implement.

How: After testing, list the problems found or potential improvements. Create a 2x2 grid (e.g., High Importance/Low Difficulty, High/High, Low/Low, Low/High). Discuss and place each item on the grid. Focus on High Importance / Low Difficulty items first!

Why: Helps prioritise changes, ensuring you work on the most impactful and achievable improvements first.

Feature Variation Models

What: Creating slightly different versions of a specific part (feature) of your prototype to test which one works best.

How: Identify a key feature you want to improve (e.g., the handle shape, the button size). Design and 3D print 2-3 variations of just that part. Test each variation with users or through simulation to see which performs better.

Why: Allows focused testing and refinement of specific design elements without rebuilding the whole prototype each time.

The "Star" spaces on Page 15 are for any other testing methods you might use.

B

Iteration Tips (Page 15)

Keep these tips in mind during testing and iteration. Write your key takeaways in the dotted lines on Page 15.

  • Focus on the User's Goal: Does the prototype help the user achieve what they need to do? That's the main measure of success.
  • Test One Big Change at a Time: If you change too many things between tests, you won't know which change caused the improvement (or problem!).
  • Failure is Learning: If your prototype breaks or doesn't work as expected, that's valuable information! Figure out *why* it failed and learn from it.
  • Record Everything: Write down observations, quotes from users, measurements, what broke, what worked well. Good notes are crucial for making smart changes.
  • Refer Back to Your Criteria: Check if your prototype is meeting the 'Must Have' criteria you set on Page 11. Use feedback to get closer to meeting them.
C

Documentation Strategy (Page 15)

How will you record your testing results and the changes you make? Plan this and write it in the box on Page 15.

Think about:

  • What data to collect: User quotes? Observation notes? Photos/videos of testing? Success/failure rates? Measurements? Scores against criteria?
  • Tools: Observation sheets? Video camera? Survey forms (digital or paper)? Your workbook?
  • Organisation: How will you keep track of feedback for Iteration 1 vs. Iteration 2? How will you summarise the key findings?

Example Sentence Starters for your Plan:

  • "During user testing, we will record [Type of data, e.g., observations and direct quotes] using [Tool, e.g., a shared document]."
  • "We plan to [Action, e.g., video record the user interaction (with permission)] to review later."
  • "Key findings from each test will be summarised using [Method, e.g., bullet points focusing on problems and successes]."
  • "Changes made for the next iteration will be documented with [Method, e.g., labelled sketches and notes] in the workbook."

TEST + ITERATE: ITERATION CYCLES

This section explains the process for documenting each **Iteration Cycle** on Workbook Pages 16, 17, and 18. An iteration is one round of testing and improving.

1

Understanding the Iteration Cycle

Each iteration page (16, 17, 18) follows a similar pattern: You show what you tested, summarise what you learned, and show how you improved it for the *next* round. Think of it like this:

Step 1: Document the 'Before' (Top of Page 16/17/18)

In the top section (with image placeholders):

  • Sketch or add photos of the prototype version you are *about to test*.
  • Briefly describe this version. What specific feature or aspect are you testing in this iteration?
  • Example: "Iteration 1: Testing the initial handle shape for comfort and grip."
Step 2: Summarise the Test & Feedback (Middle Box on Page 16/17/18)

In the rectangular box below the first images:

  • Describe *how* you tested it (e.g., "User testing with Sarah," "Roleplay simulation using gloves").
  • Summarise the key findings: What worked well? What problems occurred? What feedback did you get? Be specific!
  • Example: "Tested with Sarah. She found the handle too thin and hard to grip securely. It slipped when twisting. The overall size was good. Suggestion: Make handle thicker and add texture."
Step 3: Document the 'After' - The Improvement (Bottom of Page 16/17/18)

Based on the feedback, you make changes! In the bottom section (with image placeholders):

  • Sketch or add photos of the *new, improved* version of the prototype. Highlight the changes.
  • Explain *what* you changed and *why* based on the feedback. This becomes the version you'll test in the *next* iteration (if you do one).
  • Example: "Iteration 1 Changes: Increased handle diameter by 5mm. Added cross-hatch texture using CAD software for better grip. Ready for Iteration 2 testing."

You repeat this entire process for each iteration page you use in your workbook (Page 16 for the first test, Page 17 for the second, etc.). Not every project needs three full iterations – do as many as needed to get a good result!

TEST + ITERATE: FINAL SUMMARY & REFLECTION

After completing your testing and iteration cycles, it's time to summarise your final design and reflect on the process using Workbook Page 19.

1

Final Product Summary (Page 19)

This is where you describe the final version of your assistive technology product after all the improvements.

A

Writing the Summary

Think about:

  • What is the final product called?
  • What specific problem does it solve for your user?
  • How does it work in simple terms?
  • What makes it suitable for your user (consider ergonomics, ease of use, aesthetics)?
  • How did the iteration process improve it from the initial prototype?

Example Sentence Starters for the Summary Box on Page 19:

  • "Our final assistive technology is the 'GripEasy Jar Opener'."
  • "It is designed to help [User Name] independently open standard-sized jars, addressing their challenge with limited grip strength."
  • "The device works by..."
  • "Through testing and iteration, we improved the handle comfort and ensured it requires minimal force to operate."
2

Key Features (Page 19)

List the most important and distinct features of your final product. These should directly relate to how it helps the user and meets the design criteria.

B

Identifying Key Features

Think about what makes your design special or effective. Aim for 3-5 clear, specific features.

Examples based on the 'GripEasy Jar Opener':

Feature 1: Ergonomically shaped handle designed for comfortable, low-force grip.
Feature 2: Textured grip surface on handle to prevent slipping.
Feature 3: Internal clamp mechanism fits standard 7-9cm jar lids securely.
Feature 4: Single-piece 3D printed design for simplicity and durability.
Feature 5: Wide base provides stability during use.

Write your product's key features in the spaces provided on Page 19.

3

Checklist & Notes (Page 19)

The final part of Page 19 is for reflecting on your evaluation process.

C

The Checklist Explained

Let's break down the checklist items:

  • "I evaluated the functionality, ergonomics, aesthetics and production methods of my prototype."
    • Functionality: Does it do the job it's supposed to do?
    • Ergonomics: Is it comfortable and easy for the user to hold and operate?
    • Aesthetics: Does it look reasonably good (or at least not off-putting)?
    • Production methods: Did you consider how it was made (e.g., 3D printing quality, assembly)? Did you check if these were suitable?
  • "I used my evaluation data to develop improved iterations of my prototype."
    • Did the feedback you collected during testing *actually* lead to specific changes and improvements in your later versions? (This is the core of iteration!)

Tick the boxes on Page 19 if you feel you completed these steps effectively.

D

Making Final Notes

Use the 'Notes' box on Page 19 for any final thoughts on the entire Test and Iterate process.

You could write about:

  • The most surprising feedback you received.
  • What you learned about the design process through iteration.
  • Any ideas for future improvements beyond this project.
  • How well the final product met the initial user needs.
