IDEATION: METHODS & TIPS

This section helps you understand and choose brainstorming methods, remember useful tips, and plan how to record your ideas, all for Workbook Page 9.

Ideation is about generating lots of creative solutions to the design challenge you defined earlier.

1

Choosing Your Brainstorming Methods (Page 9)

Your workbook lists several ways to brainstorm. Understanding them helps you pick the best ones for your team. Tick the ones you'll use on Page 9.

A

Ideation Methods Explained

Icon Sketching

What: Quickly drawing simple pictures or symbols for ideas.

How: Everyone grabs markers and paper (or sticky notes). Set a timer (5-10 min). Draw as many solution ideas as possible. Use basic shapes, arrows, labels. It's about speed and visual communication, not perfect art.

Why: Fast, helps visualise concepts, great for people who think visually, can be less intimidating than writing.

Group Brainstorm

What: The team calls out ideas spontaneously while one person writes them all down where everyone can see.

How: Gather around a whiteboard or large paper. Choose a scribe. Everyone calls out ideas related to the design challenge. Key rules: no criticism allowed, encourage wild ideas, build on others' thoughts ("Yes, and..."), aim for a high number of ideas.

Why: High energy, builds team collaboration, allows ideas to spark off each other quickly.

Priority Diagramming

What: Organising generated ideas on a chart based on factors like importance, feasibility (how easy it is to make), or user impact.

How: After brainstorming, write each distinct idea on a separate sticky note. Draw axes on a board (e.g., "Impact on User" vs. "Effort to Build"). Discuss as a team where each idea fits and place the notes on the chart.

Why: Helps visualise the potential of different ideas, aids in deciding which concepts to explore further, good for initial sorting.

Dot Voting

What: A quick, democratic way for the team to show preference for certain ideas after brainstorming.

How: Display all the ideas clearly (e.g., sticky notes on a wall). Give each team member a small number of dots (e.g., 3-5 stickers or marker dots). Everyone places their dots on the ideas they think are the strongest or most promising, without discussion initially. Tally the dots.

Why: Quick consensus building, gives everyone an equal voice, helps narrow down options efficiently.

Remember to also consider the blank spaces on page 9 to add any other methods your team uses!

B

Ideation Tips (Page 9)

These tips help make brainstorming more effective. Write them (or similar ones) in the dotted lines on Page 9.

  • Defer Judgement: Don't criticise ideas during brainstorming (yours or others'). Just get them out! Analysis comes later.
  • Encourage Wild Ideas: Sometimes the craziest ideas spark the most innovative solutions. It's easier to tone down a wild idea than to make a boring one exciting.
  • Build on the Ideas of Others: Use "Yes, and..." thinking. Listen to teammates and see how you can combine or improve their concepts.
  • Stay Focused on the Topic: Keep the main design challenge ("How Might We...") in mind to ensure ideas are relevant.
  • One Conversation at a Time: Avoid side conversations during group brainstorms so everyone can hear and build on ideas.
  • Be Visual: Sketch! Draw! Use diagrams! Visuals communicate complex ideas quickly.
  • Go for Quantity: Aim for a large number of ideas. The more ideas you generate, the higher the chance of finding a truly great one.
C

Documentation Strategy (Page 9)

How will you capture all these ideas? Plan it out and write it in the box on Page 9.

Consider:

  • Tools: Sticky notes? Whiteboard? Shared digital document (like OneNote or Google Docs)? Notebooks? Camera?
  • Process: Who will write/draw? Will you take photos? How will you ensure every idea is captured?
  • Storage: How will you keep the ideas safe and accessible for the next steps?

Example Sentence Starters for your Plan:

  • "We will use [Tool, e.g., large sticky notes] during our [Method, e.g., group brainstorm]."
  • "[Person's Name or Role, e.g., The Scribe] will be responsible for writing/drawing each idea clearly."
  • "After the session, we will [Action, e.g., take photos of the whiteboard/notes]."
  • "The ideas will be stored [Location, e.g., in our shared OneNote project folder]."

IDEATION: CAPTURE IDEAS

This section guides you through documenting your top ideas and choosing the key one for Workbook Page 10.

1

Documenting Your Top Ideas (Page 10)

After brainstorming and maybe doing some initial sorting (like dot voting or priority diagramming), select your team's top 4 most promising ideas. Page 10 provides space to sketch or describe each one clearly.

A

Sketching & Describing Clearly

The goal here is communication. Someone unfamiliar with your idea should be able to understand its basic concept from your entry on Page 10.

Sketching Tips:

  • Use clear outlines.
  • Show scale if important (e.g., next to a hand).
  • Use labels and arrows for key parts.
  • Consider a simple sequence showing how it works.
  • Add brief text notes for explanation.

Describing Tips:

  • Start with a clear name or title for the idea.
  • Explain *what* it does for the user.
  • Describe *how* it generally works.
  • Mention key features or unique aspects.
  • Keep it concise and focused.

Use the four boxes on Page 10 to capture your top ideas using these techniques.

2

Summarising and Choosing (Page 10)

Page 10 also asks you to summarise your pool of ideas and then identify the single 'Key Idea' your team will pursue.

B

Summary of Ideas

Look back at all the ideas you generated, not just the top 4. What overall approaches did your team explore? What common problems were you trying to solve?

Use sentence starters like these for the 'Summary' box on Page 10:

  • "Our brainstorming explored solutions for [main user challenge], including ideas related to..."
  • "Common strategies in our ideas involved using [mechanism/approach] or changing [aspect of task]."
  • "We generated ideas ranging from simple modifications like [example] to more complex devices like [example]."
C

The Key Idea

This is the big decision! Discuss as a team and select the ONE idea you believe is the most promising to develop into a prototype.

Think about:

  • Which idea best addresses the user's core need (from your Define phase)?
  • Which seems most feasible to build within the project constraints (time, materials, skills)?
  • Which one genuinely excites your team?

Describe this chosen Key Idea clearly in the final box on Page 10. Explain *why* you chose it.

  • "Our Key Idea is [Name/Concept of Idea]."
  • "We selected this because it strongly addresses [User Need] and seems achievable with [Available Resources/Skills]."
  • "It works by [Brief explanation of mechanism/function]."
  • "We believe this has the potential to significantly improve [User's Goal/Experience]."

IDEATION: REFINE CRITERIA

This final Ideation step helps you define success for your chosen Key Idea using Workbook Page 11. You'll prioritise features and reflect on the process.

1

Refining Design Criteria (Page 11 Chart)

You have your Key Idea. Now, what features or requirements *must* it have to be successful for your user? What features *should* it have? What *could* it have if time/resources allow? Page 11's chart helps you prioritise these **Design Criteria**.

A

Understanding the Criteria Chart

Think of the chart on Page 11 like a target for your Key Idea's features:

  • Inner Circle (Centre): This is for the absolute **MUST-HAVE** criteria. These are essential for the solution to work for the user and solve their main problem. They should also be **Actionable** – things you can realistically achieve in this project.
  • Middle Ring(s): This area is for **SHOULD-HAVE** criteria. These are important features that would significantly improve the solution, but might be slightly less critical than the 'Must-Haves', or perhaps a bit harder to implement perfectly.
  • Outer Ring: This is for **COULD-HAVE** criteria (or 'Nice-to-Haves'). These are features that would be cool or beneficial but aren't essential for the core function. They might be harder to achieve, lower priority, or things to consider for future improvements ('long-term considerations').

How to use it: Brainstorm a list of features and requirements for your Key Idea. Write each one down (maybe on small slips of paper). Then, discuss as a team and place each feature onto the chart in your workbook based on its priority level (Must, Should, Could) and how actionable it seems.

Example: Key Idea = Easy-Grip Jar Opener

Let's say your user needs help opening jars due to weak grip strength. Your criteria might include:

  • **Placed in Inner Circle (Must Have / Actionable):** Fits standard jars (e.g., 7-9cm lids) Significantly reduces grip force needed Can be used with one hand Safe to use (no sharp edges) Can be 3D printed with school printer
  • **Placed in Middle Ring (Should Have):** Easy to clean / Washable Works on slightly different jar sizes Doesn't damage the jar lid Comfortable handle
  • **Placed in Outer Ring (Could Have / Less Actionable / Long Term):** Available in multiple colours Has a magnet to stick to fridge Opens other types of lids (e.g., bottles) Made from recycled plastic
2

Checklist & Notes (Page 11)

Finally, Page 11 asks you to reflect on your ideation process using the checklist and notes section.

B

The Checklist

This checklist helps ensure you've followed key Design Thinking steps during ideation:

  • Used divergent thinking: Did you truly brainstorm LOTS of different ideas initially, without limiting yourselves?
  • Used convergent thinking: Did you analyse those ideas and make thoughtful choices to narrow them down to your Key Idea?
  • Determined necessary design criteria: Did you think specifically about what your Key Idea *needs* to do (based on user needs) to be successful? (Like the Must/Should/Could exercise for the chart).

Tick the boxes on Page 11 honestly based on your team's process.

C

Making Notes

Use the 'Notes' box on Page 11 for final reflections on the Ideation stage.

Good things to note down:

  • Key decisions made during the criteria refinement (why is something a 'Must Have'?).
  • Any remaining questions or uncertainties about the Key Idea.
  • Initial thoughts on materials or construction for the prototype.
  • How the team worked together during ideation.
