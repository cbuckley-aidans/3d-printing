CHUNK 1: INTRODUCTION TO THE MAKE:ABLE CHALLENGE

TEACHER CHUNK
15 mins

What is the make:able Challenge?

The make:able challenge is a global competition that invites students to design and create 3D-printed assistive technology for someone with a physical disability or limitation.

1

Understanding Assistive Technology

Assistive technology helps people with disabilities:

Mobility Aids

Wheelchairs, walkers, and specialized grips to help people move around

Hearing Devices

Hearing aids and audio amplifiers to help people hear better

Adaptive Utensils

Special grips and tools to help people eat and drink independently

2

The Challenge Process

To complete the make:able challenge, you will:

1

Find a person with a genuine need

Identify someone who could benefit from an assistive device

2

Understand their challenge

Learn about the specific problems they face in daily activities

3

Design a solution using 3D printing

Create a custom device that addresses their specific needs

4

Test and refine your design

Get feedback and improve your solution based on user testing

3

Introduction Video

4

Problems with Traditional Assistive Technology

Expensive

Many assistive devices cost hundreds or thousands of dollars, making them inaccessible to many people who need them.

One-Size-Fits-All

Mass-produced devices aren't tailored to individual needs and may not work well for everyone.

Long Wait Times

It can take months to get custom devices through traditional manufacturing methods.

Difficult to Modify

Once made, traditional devices can be hard to adjust or repair if they break or need changes.

CHUNK 2: BENEFITS OF 3D PRINTING FOR ASSISTIVE TECHNOLOGY

TEACHER CHUNK
15 mins

Why 3D Printing is Revolutionary for Assistive Technology

3D printing offers unique advantages when creating assistive devices compared to traditional manufacturing methods.

1

CUSTOMIZATION

3D printing allows devices to be tailored exactly to an individual's body and needs

2

COST-EFFECTIVE

Creating custom devices with 3D printing is much cheaper than traditional manufacturing

3

RAPID PROTOTYPING

Designs can be quickly tested, modified, and printed again to find the best solution

4

LIGHTWEIGHT

3D printed objects can be strong but lightweight, making them comfortable to use

5

Customization in Detail

Why It Matters

  • Every person's body is different
  • Mass-produced items often don't fit well
  • Proper fit means better function and comfort
  • Can address very specific challenges

Custom fit = Better function

2

Cost-Effectiveness in Detail

Why It Matters

  • Traditional custom devices can cost hundreds or thousands of dollars
  • 3D printing materials are relatively inexpensive
  • Makes assistive technology accessible to more people
  • Replacements and repairs are affordable

Real-World Example

A professional prosthetic hand can cost $10,000+, but a functional 3D printed version can be made for under $50 in materials.

Lower cost = Greater access

3

Rapid Prototyping in Detail

Why It Matters

  • Can quickly test multiple design ideas
  • Get immediate feedback from users
  • Make adjustments and print again in hours, not weeks
  • Leads to more refined, effective final products

Real-World Example

A student created 5 different versions of a button-pusher device in a single week, getting feedback from the user each time to perfect the design.

Quick iterations = Better results

4

Lightweight Design in Detail

Why It Matters

  • Heavy devices can cause fatigue and discomfort
  • 3D printing can create hollow or honeycomb structures
  • Reduces material while maintaining strength
  • Especially important for devices used for extended periods

Real-World Example

A student designed a lightweight arm brace with a honeycomb structure that provided support while being comfortable enough to wear all day.

Lightweight + Strong = Comfortable use

CHUNK 3: GETTING STARTED WITH THE MAKE:ABLE CHALLENGE

TEACHER CHUNK
10 mins

The Design Thinking Process

To create a successful make:able project, you'll follow a design thinking process that keeps the user at the center of your work.

1

Design Thinking Steps

1

Empathize

Find and understand a user with a genuine need

2

Define

Clearly articulate the problem to be solved

3

Ideate

Brainstorm possible solutions

4

Prototype

Create models and test designs

5

Test & Refine

Gather feedback and improve the design

Empathize

In this phase, you'll:

  • Find a person with a disability or limitation
  • Interview them about their challenges
  • Observe how they currently handle tasks
  • Build empathy for their experience
Key Questions:
  • What everyday tasks are difficult for them?
  • What existing solutions have they tried?
  • What would make the biggest difference in their daily life?
  • What are their preferences and priorities?

Define

In this phase, you'll:

  • Analyze what you learned from your user
  • Define a clear problem statement
  • Focus on the specific need to address
  • Establish criteria for a successful solution
Problem Statement Format:

[User name] needs a way to [accomplish task] because [reason why it's important].

Example: "Sarah needs a way to hold her paintbrush steadily because her hand tremor makes it difficult to enjoy her favorite hobby."

Ideate

In this phase, you'll:

  • Brainstorm many possible solutions
  • Think creatively without limitations
  • Consider how 3D printing capabilities could help
  • Involve your user in generating ideas
Brainstorming Tips:
  • Aim for quantity over quality initially
  • Build on others' ideas
  • Avoid judging ideas too early
  • Use sketches to communicate concepts

Prototype

In this phase, you'll:

  • Select the most promising ideas to develop
  • Create simple models or mock-ups first
  • Use CAD software to design 3D models
  • 3D print initial prototypes
Prototyping Considerations:
  • Start simple and iterate
  • Consider print orientation for strength
  • Test different infill patterns/densities
  • Think about how pieces will connect

Test & Refine

In this phase, you'll:

  • Have your user test the prototype
  • Collect specific feedback
  • Make improvements based on testing
  • Repeat the process until the design works well
Refinement Questions:
  • Is it comfortable to use?
  • Does it accomplish the task effectively?
  • Is it durable enough?
  • What could be improved?
  • Are there any unexpected issues?
2

What Makes a Successful Project

Do

  • Address a real need for a specific person
  • Use 3D printing in a way that showcases its advantages
  • Keep the design simple but effective
  • Document your process and iterations
  • Include the user throughout the design process
  • Test your design with the actual user

Don't

  • Create a solution without a specific user in mind
  • Design something that could be made more easily without 3D printing
  • Make the design unnecessarily complex
  • Wait until the end to test with your user
  • Forget to document your design process
  • Give up when your first design doesn't work perfectly
3

Finding a User

Think about potential users you could design for:

Family Members

Do you have relatives with physical limitations who could benefit from assistive technology?

School Community

Are there students or staff at your school who face physical challenges?

Community Organizations

Local community centers, religious organizations, or senior centers might connect you with potential users

Support Groups

Local organizations that support people with specific disabilities might help you connect with a user
