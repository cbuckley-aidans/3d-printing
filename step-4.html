Choosing Empathy Methods (Page 6)

How will you learn more about your user's experience? Page 6 lists several methods. Choose the ones you plan to use and tick them. Here's more about each:

A

Empathy Methods Explained

Online Research

What: Finding information online about the specific disability, challenges, existing solutions, and user experiences.

How: Use reliable sources like reputable health websites (Mayo Clinic, WebMD), disability advocacy groups (Arthritis Australia, Vision Australia), academic articles, or online forums where people share experiences. Look for facts about the condition and personal stories.

Why: Good starting point, provides background knowledge, helps you form better questions for interviews or observations.

User Interview

What: Talking directly with your end user (or someone very similar) to ask about their life, challenges, needs, and feelings.

How: Prepare open-ended questions beforehand (e.g., "Can you tell me about...", "What's that like for you?", "What works well/doesn't work?"). Listen more than you talk. Be respectful and curious. Record notes (with permission).

Why: Provides rich, detailed, personal insights directly from the source. Builds connection and understanding.

Simulations

What: Trying to experience a small part of the user's challenge yourself to build understanding.

How: Safely simulate a limitation, e.g., wear bulky gloves to mimic reduced dexterity, use Vaseline on glasses for blurred vision, try tasks using only one hand. Reflect on how it felt and what was difficult.

Why: Can offer a powerful glimpse into physical difficulties and frustrations. Must be done respectfully, knowing it's not the full experience.

Observations

What: Watching the user perform relevant tasks in their natural environment without interfering (with permission).

How: Ask the user if you can watch them do a task related to the problem (e.g., making breakfast, opening mail). Take notes on their actions, where they struggle, any workarounds they use, and the environment itself. Be unobtrusive.

Why: Reveals what people *do* (which can differ from what they *say*). Shows real-world context and challenges they might not even think to mention.

Challenge Map

What: Visually mapping out the steps of a task or a day, highlighting the specific challenges the user faces at each point.

How: Break down a relevant activity (e.g., "Getting Ready in the Morning") into small steps. For each step, note down the difficulties the user experiences based on your research/interviews/observations. You could use sticky notes or a flowchart.

Why: Helps visualise the sequence of challenges, identifies key pain points, and shows where interventions might be most helpful.

Empathy Map

What: A tool to organise your understanding of the user into four areas: What they SAY, THINK, DO, and FEEL.

How: Draw four quadrants on paper labelled Says, Thinks, Does, Feels. Fill in each section with observations and inferences based on your research. 'Says' = direct quotes. 'Does' = observed actions. 'Thinks' & 'Feels' = your educated guesses about their internal state (e.g., "Thinks: 'This is frustrating'", "Feels: Determined").

Why: Provides a holistic view of the user, helps identify contradictions (e.g., says one thing, does another), considers emotional aspects.

The "Star" spaces on Page 6 are for any other empathy methods you might use.

2

Identifying Insights (Page 7)

Insights are the 'Aha!' moments. They are discoveries about your user's underlying needs or motivations that can inspire design solutions. They often connect different pieces of information you gathered.

Page 7 asks you to document 4 key insights.

A

What Makes a Good Insight?

An insight isn't just an observation (e.g., "Alex struggles to open jars"). It explains the *why* or the *meaning* behind the observation.

  • It often reveals a tension or unmet need.
  • It connects behaviour with motivation or feeling.
  • It should feel like a discovery that could lead to new ideas.
  • It's specific to your user's context.
Finding Insights: Ask 'Why?'

Observation: "Alex waits for help to open jars."

Ask Why? -> "Because their hands hurt and they lack strength."

Ask Why is that important? -> "Because they value independence and feel frustrated asking for help with simple tasks."

Potential Insight: "Alex needs a way to open jars independently to maintain their sense of self-reliance and avoid the daily frustration of dependence, even though their hand strength is limited."

B

Documenting Your Insights (Page 7)

For each of the 4 insight sections on Page 7:

  • Image Area: You could sketch a scene representing the insight, draw the user's emotion, or find/create a simple icon or image that captures the essence.
  • Text Area: Write the insight statement clearly. Use the format "[User] needs [Need] because [Reason/Motivation/Underlying Cause]."

Example Insight Statements:

  • "Alex needs a way to manage daily tasks requiring grip strength without feeling like they are using a 'medical device' because maintaining a sense of normalcy is important to their identity."
  • "Sarah needs clear visual and tactile feedback when using kitchen tools because her reduced sensation makes it hard to know if she's using them correctly or safely."
  • "Ben needs a method to carry small items while using his walker because having his hands free allows him to feel more stable and confident when moving around."

1

Challenge Framing & "How Might We..." (Page 8)

Challenge framing turns your insights about user needs into actionable questions that guide your ideation (idea generation) phase.

A

Understanding "How Might We..." Statements

A "How Might We..." (HMW) question is a powerful way to frame your challenge because it:

  • How: Suggests solutions exist.
  • Might: Implies possibility and encourages exploration (doesn't demand a single right answer).
  • We: Highlights the collaborative nature of finding a solution.

A good HMW statement should be inspired by your insights, specific enough to provide focus, but broad enough to allow for creative ideas.

Structure: How Might We [Action Verb] for [User] so that [Desired Outcome / Benefit linked to Insight]?

Too Broad: How might we help Alex?

(Doesn't specify the problem area)

Too Narrow / Solution-Based: How might we make a better jar opener handle for Alex?

(Already suggests the solution - 'jar opener handle' - limiting creativity)

Just Right: How might we enable Alex to open jars independently so that they feel more self-reliant in the kitchen?

(Specifies user, action, and desired outcome linked to an insight about independence)

Just Right: How might we provide Sarah with clear feedback when using kitchen tools so that she feels confident and safe while cooking?

(Specifies user, need for feedback, and desired outcome linked to safety/confidence insight)

Use your strongest insight from Page 7 to craft your main HMW question in the large central space on Page 8. The smaller boxes around it might be for alternative HMWs based on other insights, or breaking down the main HMW.

2

Initial Design Criteria (Page 8)

Design Criteria are the requirements or goals your solution needs to meet to be considered successful. They come directly from your user's needs and your insights.

Page 8 asks for 6 initial criteria.

B

Defining Initial Criteria

Think about what your solution MUST do or be like. Consider both:

  • Functional Criteria: How it works, what it achieves (e.g., "Reduces force needed by X%", "Holds item securely").
  • Non-Functional Criteria: Qualities like ease of use, comfort, safety, appearance, durability, cost, manufacturability (e.g., "Easy to clean," "Comfortable to hold," "Doesn't look 'medical'," "Can be 3D printed").
Turning Insights/Needs into Criteria

Need/Insight: "Alex needs to open jars independently to feel self-reliant."

Potential Criteria:**

  • Allows single-handed operation.
  • Requires minimal grip strength (quantify later if possible).
  • Successfully opens standard jars 9/10 times.
  • Is intuitive to use without instructions.
  • Durable enough for daily use.
  • Aesthetically acceptable for kitchen counter.

Write 6 specific criteria for your project in the spaces provided on Page 8. Make them clear and measurable if possible.
