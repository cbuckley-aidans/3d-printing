CHUNK 1: INTRODUCTION TO EMPATHY IN DESIGN

TEACHER CHUNK
15 mins

Understanding Empathy in the Design Process

Empathy means understanding how other people feel and experience the world. It's a key part of designing products that truly help people with disabilities.

1

Empathy vs. Sympathy

Sympathy

  • Feeling for someone
  • Staying at a distance
  • Often leads to pity
  • May make assumptions
  • Focuses on limitations
"That's too bad"

Empathy

  • Feeling with someone
  • Trying to understand their experience
  • Creates connection
  • Based on research and listening
  • Sees the whole person
"I'm trying to understand"

2

Why Empathy Matters

Better Solutions

Designs that come from empathy actually meet users' real needs, not what we guess they need

User Dignity

Products made with empathy respect users' abilities and independence, not just focus on their limitations

Innovation

Really understanding users often leads to surprising and creative solutions

3

Design With vs. Without Empathy

Design Examples Example 1 of 2

Without vs. With Empathy: Can Openers

Without Empathy

Standard Can Opener: Requires fine motor control and hand strength, making it difficult for users with arthritis or hand limitations.

Problem: Designed assuming all users have typical hand dexterity and strength.

With Empathy

Easy-Grip Can Opener: Designed with a lever mechanism that requires less strength and precision.

Improvement: Designed after understanding the challenges faced by users with limited hand strength.

Without vs. With Empathy: Water Bottles

Without Empathy

Standard Water Bottle: Requires a strong grip to open and may be difficult to hold for someone with limited hand mobility.

Problem: Designs that prioritize looks or cost over accessibility.

With Empathy

Accessible Water Bottle: Features a design with a handle and drinking tube that works for users with different abilities.

Improvement: Designed after observing users struggle with standard bottles.

4

Hear from End Users

Listening to people with disabilities talk about their experiences is one of the most powerful ways to develop empathy. Pay attention to both the challenges they describe and the solutions they've already created for themselves.

Why Empathy Matters in Design

1

BETTER PRODUCTS

Products designed with empathy solve real needs rather than assumed ones

2

USER DIGNITY

Empathetic design respects the whole person and their independence

3

INNOVATIVE SOLUTIONS

Understanding users deeply often leads to unexpected creative solutions

4

INCLUSIVE DESIGN

Empathy helps us create products that work for diverse users with many abilities

Activity Goals

1

Empathy Methods Explorer

Click each method to learn more:

Online Research

What it is:

Looking for information online about specific disabilities, existing solutions, and user experiences.

Example:

Researching arthritis to understand how it affects hand movements and reading forums where people with arthritis discuss their daily challenges.

Pros:
  • Easy to start with
  • Lots of information available
  • Can do before meeting your user
  • Helps you ask better questions later
Cons:
  • Not specific to your user
  • Information may not be reliable
  • Might lead to wrong assumptions
  • Not as good as talking to someone directly
Best Resources:
  • Medical websites (Mayo Clinic, WebMD)
  • Disability organization websites
  • Forums and social media groups
  • YouTube videos of people sharing experiences
  • Academic research papers

User Interview

What it is:

Having a conversation with your user to understand their experiences, challenges, and needs.

Example:

Sitting down with a user who has limited hand mobility and asking about their daily routines, challenges, and how they've adapted.

Pros:
  • Get information directly from your user
  • Builds relationship and trust
  • Can ask follow-up questions
  • Hear about challenges in their own words
Cons:
  • People may not always know what they need
  • Needs good questions prepared in advance
  • Can take a lot of time
  • Some users may find it hard to talk about challenges
Good Interview Questions:
  • "Can you walk me through your typical day?"
  • "What activities do you find most challenging?"
  • "What solutions have you tried before?"
  • "What do you wish people understood about your experience?"
  • "What would make the biggest difference in your daily life?"

Simulations

What it is:

Trying to experience aspects of a disability yourself to better understand the physical or sensory challenges.

Example:

Wearing thick gloves to simulate limited hand dexterity and then trying to use everyday objects like pens, scissors, or zippers.

Pros:
  • Gives you first-hand experience of challenges
  • Helps identify issues you might not think to ask about
  • Makes the experience more real
  • Can reveal insights about existing products
Cons:
  • Only simulates physical aspects, not the whole lived experience
  • Can be too simplified
  • May create misunderstandings about the disability
  • Should be done respectfully
Common Simulations:
  • Thick gloves for hand mobility limitations
  • Glasses smeared with Vaseline for vision impairment
  • Earplugs for hearing impairment
  • Using only one hand for tasks
  • Using a wheelchair to navigate spaces

Observations

What it is:

Watching your user as they do activities in their normal environment without interrupting them.

Example:

Observing someone with limited mobility as they prepare a meal, noticing which tasks are difficult and what adaptations they've already made.

Pros:
  • See actual behavior, not just what people say
  • Notice workarounds and adaptations
  • Identify challenges the user may not mention
  • Understand environmental factors
Cons:
  • Can feel intrusive
  • Takes time
  • People may act differently when being watched
  • Requires permission and trust
What to Look For:
  • Points where they have difficulty or hesitate
  • Creative adaptations already in use
  • Environmental factors that help or hinder
  • Emotional responses (frustration, satisfaction)
  • How much time and effort tasks require

Challenge Map

What it is:

Creating a visual map of challenges your user faces throughout their day or during specific activities.

Example:

Mapping out all the steps involved in cooking a meal, identifying pain points and rating the difficulty of each step for your user.

Pros:
  • Creates a visual overview of challenges
  • Helps prioritize which problems to solve
  • Shows connections between different challenges
  • Can do with your user to check understanding
Cons:
  • Requires prior knowledge about the user's routine
  • Can be complex for multiple activities
  • Might miss challenges if your mapping isn't detailed enough
  • Needs verification from the user
How to Create:
  • List activities in order of when they happen
  • Break activities into detailed steps
  • Mark each step as easy/medium/difficult
  • Note specific challenges at each step
  • Identify environmental factors that may impact difficulty

Empathy Map

What it is:

A visual tool that helps you organize what you've learned about your user in four categories: what they say, do, think, and feel.

Example:

Creating a chart with four sections about a user with arthritis: what they say about their condition, what you observe them doing, what you think they're thinking, and how they might be feeling.

Pros:
  • Organizes information in a structured way
  • Considers both observable and emotional aspects
  • Helps identify differences between what people say and do
  • Creates a shareable document of user insights
Cons:
  • Requires guesses about thoughts and feelings
  • Needs verification from the user
  • Might oversimplify complex experiences
  • Works best after gathering other data
Empathy Map Sections:
  • SAYS: Quotes and statements from the user
  • DOES: Actions and behaviors you observe
  • THINKS: What they might be thinking
  • FEELS: Emotions they might experience
  • Sometimes includes "Pains" and "Gains" sections

CHUNK 2: UNDERSTANDING AND DOCUMENTING USER NEEDS

TEACHER CHUNK
15 mins

Effective Documentation of Empathy Research

Recording what you learn about your user is essential. Good documentation helps you remember key insights and share them with others.

1

Asking Effective Questions

Good questions lead to better insights about your user's needs:

Ineffective Questions

Closed: "Do you have trouble opening jars?"

Only allows yes/no answers, doesn't explore details

Leading: "Don't you find it frustrating when you can't open packages?"

Suggests the answer and emotional response

Too General: "What's it like having a disability?"

Too broad to get specific, useful insights

Solution-Focused: "Would a jar opener with a bigger handle help you?"

Jumps to solutions before understanding the problem

Effective Questions

Open-Ended: "Could you describe your experience with kitchen tasks?"

Encourages detailed responses

Specific: "What's your process for opening a jar of sauce?"

Focuses on concrete examples and processes

Behavior-Based: "What do you do when you encounter a package you can't open?"

Reveals workarounds and adaptations

Emotion-Exploring: "How do you feel when you need to ask for help with everyday tasks?"

Uncovers emotional needs and priorities

2

Documentation Methods

Documentation Methods Method 1 of 3

Written Notes

Best Practices:
  • Use the user's exact words when possible (direct quotes)
  • Separate observations from your interpretations
  • Include date, time, and context
  • Note non-verbal cues (facial expressions, tone of voice)
  • Organize by themes or activities
When to Use:
  • During interviews
  • While observing
  • For recording quick insights
  • When other recording methods aren't appropriate

Pro Tip: Create a template beforehand with sections for different types of information (quotes, observations, questions, insights) to help organize your notes as you take them.

Photos & Videos

Best Practices:
  • Always get permission before recording
  • Focus on activities and environments, not just the person
  • Capture the "before, during, after" of challenging tasks
  • Take photos of existing tools, adaptations, and workarounds
  • Add notes to photos to explain context
When to Use:
  • To capture complex processes
  • To show environmental context
  • When words alone don't convey the situation
  • To record demonstrations of tasks

Pro Tip: Create a short video of your user performing a challenging task, then review it together to get their commentary on what's happening and how they feel during each step.

Visual Maps & Diagrams

Best Practices:
  • Use consistent symbols and color coding
  • Include a legend for your symbols
  • Focus on relationships between elements
  • Rate severity of pain points (e.g., 1-5 scale)
  • Review with your user to verify accuracy
When to Use:
  • To show connections between activities
  • To map processes and identify pain points
  • To organize complex information
  • When collaborating with others

Pro Tip: Create your maps digitally or with sticky notes so you can easily reorganize information as you learn more about your user's experiences.


3

Finding Patterns & Insights

After collecting information, you need to find patterns and extract meaningful insights:

1

Organize Your Data

Group similar information together by themes, activities, or types of challenges.

Example: Group all notes about "kitchen activities," "personal care," and "mobility" separately.

2

Look for Patterns

Identify recurring challenges, workarounds, or preferences across different activities.

Example: Notice that grip strength is a challenge in multiple activities: opening jars, holding utensils, and turning doorknobs.

3

Identify Contradictions

Note when what a user says contradicts their behavior or when there are conflicting needs.

Example: User says independence is most important but hesitates to use assistive devices in public due to concerns about appearance.

4

Distinguish Explicit vs. Implicit Needs

Recognize both stated needs and unstated needs you observe.

Example:
Explicit: "I need something to help me open jars."
Implicit: User wants solutions that don't look medical or draw attention to their disability.

5

Prioritize Insights

Determine which needs have the greatest impact on the user's quality of life.

Example: Rank insights based on frequency (occurs daily), severity (causes pain), and emotional impact (causes frustration or embarrassment).

Step Inside Thinking Routine

What might they know or believe?

Questions to Consider:
  • What knowledge do they have about their condition?
  • What do they believe is possible or impossible for them?
  • What experiences have shaped their beliefs?
  • What assumptions might they make about new tools or technologies?
  • What might they know about existing solutions?
Sentence Starters:
  • "They might believe that..."

What might they care about?

Consider their priorities, values, and emotional needs:

Questions to Consider:

  • What emotions might they experience throughout the day?
  • What might cause them anxiety, frustration, or joy?
  • How might they feel about asking for or receiving help?
  • What aspects of their identity might be important to them?

Empathy Tips

CHUNK 3: FRAMING DESIGN CHALLENGES

TEACHER CHUNK
10 mins

From Empathy to Design Opportunities

After gathering empathy insights, you need to frame a specific design challenge that will guide your solution.

1

The "How Might We" Format

The "How Might We" (HMW) format is a powerful way to frame design challenges because it:

Suggests Possibility

"Might" implies there are multiple solutions

Encourages Collaboration

"We" makes it a team effort

Provides Focus

Narrows the problem to something specific

Inspires Action

Turns problems into opportunities


Format: "How might we [action verb] [specific user] to [desired outcome] [context/constraint]?"

2

Challenge Framing Examples

TOO BROAD

How might we help people with disabilities?

Problem: Too general, doesn't say which user, need, or situation.

TOO NARROW

How might we design a red pencil grip for John?

Problem: Too specific, already includes a solution (pencil grip) and limits creativity.

JUST RIGHT

How might we enable Sarah to write for extended periods without pain?

Good: Names specific user, clear need, measurable outcome, but still open to many possible solutions.

JUST RIGHT

How might we help Alex open food packages independently while maintaining dignity?

Good: Addresses both functional need (opening packages) and emotional need (dignity), specific to user.

3

Design Criteria

Design criteria define what a successful solution must achieve:

Functional Criteria

Related to how the solution works and performs

Examples:
  • "Must be operable with one hand"
  • "Should reduce required grip strength by 50%"
  • "Must be waterproof"
  • "Should weigh less than 200g"
  • "Must fit standard jar sizes (5-10cm diameter)"

Emotional Criteria

Related to how the solution makes the user feel

Examples:
  • "Should not look medical or draw attention"
  • "Must preserve user's dignity and independence"
  • "Should be aesthetically pleasing to the user"
  • "Must not cause embarrassment in public settings"
  • "Should give user confidence in their abilities"
Learn More About Creating Effective Design Criteria

When developing criteria, prioritize them as "must have" (essential), "should have" (important), and "could have" (nice to have but not essential).

CHEW 3: FROM EMPATHY TO DESIGN OPPORTUNITIES

STUDENT ACTIVITY
15 mins

Frame Your Design Challenge

Activity Goals

  • Create effective "How Might We" statements
  • Develop design criteria based on user needs
  • Get feedback to improve your challenge framing
1

Draft "How Might We" Statements

Based on what you know about your user, create 2-3 possible "How Might We" statements:

HMW Statement Guidelines

Create statements that:

  1. Name your specific user
  2. Address a clear need or challenge
  3. Include a desired outcome
  4. Are neither too broad nor too narrow
  5. Don't include a specific solution yet

Write your HMW statements in your Make:able workbook or on sticky notes.

Helpful Templates:
  • "How might we help [user] to [accomplish task] without [current limitation]?"
  • "How might we enable [user] to [meet need] in a way that [important criteria]?"
  • "How might we improve [user]'s experience of [activity] so that [desired outcome]?"
2

Peer Feedback

Share your HMW statements with a small group for feedback:

Feedback Process

  1. Form groups of 3-4 students
  2. Take turns sharing your HMW statements
  3. For each statement, group members should provide feedback using these criteria:

Is it specific to the user's needs?

Is it neither too broad nor too narrow?

Does it allow for multiple possible solutions?

Is it actionable?

Use the feedback to improve your HMW statement. Choose the strongest one to move forward with.

3

Develop Design Criteria

Based on your user research and challenge statement, develop criteria for your solution:

Initial Design Criteria

Develop 4-6 criteria that your solution should meet, using these prompts:

MUST

The solution must... (essential requirements)

SHOULD

The solution should... (important but not essential)

COULD

It would be beneficial if the solution could... (nice to have features)

Include both functional criteria (how it works) and emotional criteria (how it makes the user feel).

Record your design criteria in your Make:able workbook on page 8-9.
